Background

Education

I was born in Oregon, but went to primary and secondary school in Southern California. Every summer I returned to visit my relatives in Portland, Oregon. Due to my love of nature found from the Pacific Northwest, I started my post-secondary school education with a Bachelors of Science in Biological Sciences from the University of California at Irvine. I excelled at organic chemistry, the class used as a filter to weed out students who couldn't handle the academic rigor. The focus of the content was about explaining why chemical reactions happen the way they do based on the functional parts of chemical compounds. I found this topic highly engaging, whereas the standard Biology courses were emphasizing memorization of long chains of chemical reactions without much care for the reasons. I ended up frequently tutoring my peers in my study group, because I was able to explain the reasonings of concepts clearly and precisely in way that facilitates understanding rather than rote memorization.

After graduating, I took a year off by teaching English abroad at a public school in South Korea. I found joy in helping people build knowledge. I decided to pursue being a certified teacher and attended the University of California Los Angeles for a Masters in Education. After finishing the program, I went back to South Korea, where I taught at middle school science at an international school in Seoul.

Why Programming?

After more than a few years teaching Middle School Science preaching the growth mindset day in and day out, I decided to take a dose of my own medicine. I wanted to challenge myself to learn something I struggled with back in high school -- computer programming. According to the gospel I've been preaching to my students, I'll get it, sooner or later if I keep trying. Have a beginner's mindset. Try. Fail. Rest. Try again later. Keep this up and things will eventually form meaning. As long as you are better than you were before, that's growth. I first tried out Harvard's CS50 course. I felt that it was a lot of theory, and not enough direct practice. My cousin Byron suggested I try out Epicodus' curriculum, since he had success from there earlier. I started teaching myself part time, whenever I could off of work, with Epicodus favorited on my bookmark. After a while, I found that computer programming offered something that was not so certain in teaching. What you put in is what you reap in rewards. Teaching middle school students did not always pan out that way. So here I am, going down the rabbit hole of computer proramming.
